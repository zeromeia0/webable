Learn finance basics

Plain-language explanations for beginners. Nothing here is financial advice. Past returns do not predict the future; markets change.

A. Investment types

Bonds

  • What: Loans you make to governments or companies; they pay interest and return principal later.
  • How it works: You receive coupons; the bond’s price moves with interest rates.
  • Main risk: Inflation, issuer default (rarer on high-quality government bonds), rising rates lowering bond prices.
  • Typical long-term range (very rough): Often lower single-digit annual returns — not guaranteed.
  • Who it may suit: People wanting steadier income and willing to accept modest returns.

Beginner warning: “Safe” does not mean risk-free; long maturities can still lose value if rates rise.

Stock indexes

  • What: A basket tracking many companies (e.g. a broad market index).
  • How it works: You buy diversification in one product; returns follow the average of that basket.
  • Main risk: Market crashes, long drawdowns, currency exposure if not hedged.
  • Typical long-term range (very rough): Historically equities have often beaten bonds over multi-decade periods — not guaranteed every decade.
  • Who it may suit: Long horizons willing to ride volatility.

Beginner warning: Index funds still drop sharply in recessions; past averages can change.

Individual stocks

  • What: Ownership slices of one company.
  • How it works: Share price reflects expectations, earnings, and sentiment.
  • Main risk: Company-specific failure, sector shocks, concentration.
  • Typical long-term range: Extremely wide — big winners and total losers both exist.
  • Who it may suit: People who enjoy research and accept binary outcomes.

Beginner warning: Picking stocks is harder than it looks; most beginners under-diversify.

Crypto

  • What: Digital assets on blockchains (e.g. Bitcoin, Ethereum).
  • How it works: Supply/demand, network usage, regulation, liquidity.
  • Main risk: Extreme volatility, exchange risk, regulatory change; total loss is possible.
  • Typical long-term range: Unpredictable; treat as high-risk satellite, not core emergency savings.
  • Who it may suit: Only people who can afford to lose the stake.

Beginner warning: Crypto can move faster than traditional assets; never borrow to buy crypto.

B. Budgeting systems

1. Zero-based budgeting

Definition: Income minus every planned euro (expenses + savings + goals) equals zero — every euro has a job.

  • Pros: maximum awareness; less “mystery spending”.
  • Cons: time-intensive; very rigid months can feel stressful.
  • Best for: detail-oriented people fixing overspending.

Example: €2,000 in → €900 rent, €300 groceries, €200 transport, €400 savings, €200 fun… until €0 left unassigned.

2. Envelope system

Definition: Split cash or accounts into category “envelopes” with hard caps.

  • Pros: strong guardrails; easy to see when an envelope is empty.
  • Cons: moving money between envelopes can get fiddly.
  • Best for: impulse spenders who need visible limits.

Example: €150 “Dining out” envelope — when it hits €0, you wait until next month.

3. Pay yourself first

Definition: Automate savings/investments when you are paid; live on what remains.

  • Pros: builds wealth on autopilot; simple mentally.
  • Cons: can feel tight if fixed costs rise without review.
  • Best for: steady earners prioritising long-term goals.

Example: Day 1: €300 to savings + €200 to investments, then bills from what’s left.
